editorial and opinion articles
An editorial or opinion article is an opinionated news story written by a member of the editorial board of a newspaper. The editor doesn't just rant their opinions to the public. Rather, they present a timely news story and a clear, fact-supported argument, much like a thesis. Naturally, the editor is allowed to show their bias in an editorial article, because their purpose is to persuade people to believe what they believe. They get the freedom to piece together news stories and make sense of current events in their own perspective. Us readers must choose for ourselves whether we side with the editor or not.

Most editorial articles have a disclaimer at the bottom saying that the publication does not affiliate itself with the editor's views, but this is not always true

Typical posts involve long-form discussions of various topics, including artificial intelligence, outer space, and procrastination, using a combination of prose and rough illustrations." -- Wikipedia
"Blog" comes from the word "web-log". Blog posts present the author's ideas and experiences to spark conversation between the author and the reader. They are personal, biased, and more anecdotal than factual. Often you'll see blogs have comment boxes at the bottom where readers interact with each other and with the author. Blogs are not like Twitter or Instagram posts. Twitter threads are not for productive conversation, but for virtual fistfights and mad roasts. Since blogs are possible sources of income, a serious blogger must keep their writing concise to attract readers. Bloggers have to always think of their target market. So if I were to post something like, "I hope *** dies this year", I'd go on Twitter, not on an informative blog that pays my bills. And if I were running a satirical blog, I'd be more creative — again, always thinking of the target market.

fiction
Without bias, we would not have literature. To be able to express ourselves, we need to use our unique opinions and biases about this world. Yes, bias can be a good thing. Since bias is inherent in fiction, like all other examples here, it is never to be taken as hard fact. A novel doesn't need to come with a bibliography, because the author is the only citation it needs. Even historical fiction is not free from bias. The author does not want to be impartially truthful, but authentic.

Here is a Cliffsnotes summary of the classic novel, Lord of the Flies by William Golding.
Lord of the Flies at a Glance

In Lord of the Flies, British schoolboys are stranded on a tropical island. In an attempt to recreate the culture they left behind, they elect Ralph to lead, with the intellectual Piggy as counselor. But Jack wants to lead, too, and one-by-one, he lures the boys from civility and reason to the savage survivalism of primeval hunters. In Lord of the Flies, William Golding gives us a glimpse of the savagery that underlies even the most civilized human beings.
The three most important aspects of Lord of the Flies:
  • The major theme of Lord of the Flies is that humans are essentially barbaric if not downright evil. The stranded boys begin by establishing a society similar to the one they left behind in England, but soon their society has degenerated into rival clans ruled by fear and violence; before the book is over, three boys have been killed.
  • The novel is an allegory, which is a story in which characters, settings, and events stand for things larger than themselves. For example, the island represents the world; Ralph and Jack symbolize different approaches to leadership.
  • William Golding wrote Lord of the Flies following World War II, during which the Nazis exterminated six million Jews and the United States dropped two atomic bombs on Japan. In this context, the novel's profound pessimism is understandable.
