Bias is unacceptable in news content. Media outlets have a duty to disperse information unbiasedly, so that the citizens of this country become informed and able to make good decisions. When news content is affected by bias, our citizens may suffer.

In the example boxes, click on the gray highlights to navigate to the transcript snippets.

This page will focus on a recent news story, and how it was covered by the three media giants in the Philippines: TV 5, ABS-CBN, and GMA, on their respective evening programs. Watch the three videos below, or see the transcripts.

A few days before, the Presidential Security Guard was reported to have recieved vaccines even though the FDA had not approved any vaccines yet. There is no law that incriminates one who gets injected with an unapproved vaccine, but smuggling unapproved vaccines into the country is most certainly illegal. The PSG confessed to the public that they--of their own accord--arranged to be vaccinated. The Congress and the Senate wished to call the PSG into their investigation on smuggled vaccines. But President Duterte reacted.

11 types of media bias
Most media/news bias articles I've encountered on the Internet use American news outlets as their examples, including this article by allsides.com, which enumerates 11 types of media bias. I wanted to showcase something similar but using our own media networks as examples. I have borrowed allsides' definitions for the types of media bias. We will be dissecting three different news reports on the story of Duterte's reaction to the PSG vaccination.

spin

Spin is a type of media bias that means vague, dramatic or sensational language. When journalists put a “spin” on a story, they stray from objective, measurable facts. Spin is a form of media bias that clouds a reader’s view, preventing them from getting a precise take on what happened.



Kumambiyo, or to suddenly shift, is dramatic language.

Journalists sometimes make claims in their reporting without including evidence to back them up. This can occur in the headline of an article, or in the body.

sensationalism/emotionalism

Sensationalism is a type of media bias in which information is presented in a way that gives a shock or makes a deep impression. Often it gives readers a false sense of culmination, that all previous reporting has led to this ultimate story.



The word "nagbanta" shocks the viewer. Duterte did not threaten the lawmakers; he only warned them of a possible "little crisis" if PSG was placed in contempt in their hearing. See the 2nd quote.In TV5's own news report, Duterte actually says that he's not threatening.

mudlinging/ad hominem

Mudslinging is a type of media bias when unfair or insulting things are said about someone in order to damage their reputation. Similarly, ad hominem (Latin for “to the person”) attacks are attacks on a person’s motive or character traits instead of the content of their argument or idea. Ad hominem attacks can be used overtly, or as a way to subtly discredit someone without having to engage with their argument.



None observed.

mind reading

Mind reading is a type of media bias that occurs in journalism when a writer assumes they know what another person thinks, or thinks that the way they see the world reflects the way the world really is.



In the quote on the right, there is no evidence that Duterte was indeed talking about the PSG when he said that. The reporter is reading Duterte's mind.

slant

Slant is a type of media bias that describes when journalists tell only part of a story. It can include cherry-picking information or data to support one side, or ignoring another perspective. Slant prevents readers from getting the full story, and narrows the scope of our understanding.



See the first two quotes below. ABS-CBN used these quotes in their report where the President seems to be indifferent to the plight of his soldiers, seeking only his own survival.

ABS-CBN

Duterte: Hayaan mo silang mamatay kung mayroong masamang epekto yung--I don't know what kind of vaccine--I don't care, bahala sila, buhay nila iyon.


ABS-CBN

Duterte: How can I survive if wala akong close-in to guard me... to prevent untoward, you know, incidents? Iyan ang problema diyan.


However, GMA used instead a different quote of Duterte where he seems to truly care about his soldiers. See this quote below.

GMA

Duterte: The lives of these people--the lives of these soldiers--are placed on jeopardy, because, walang choice eh. Trabaho nila 'yan. If the intelligence people would say, "Go to the crowd and mix with them, and maybe look for any hostile attitude or hostile act." Ang akin namang nakapalibot, babae't lalaki, hindi naman makalapit sa akin, kasi, "Sir, social distancing." How can they protect me if "social distancing"?


See the stark difference between the last two quotes, though they have essentially the same meaning. ABS-CBN's quote of Duterte has wrong grammar, whereas GMA's quote of Duterte portrays him like a hero.

ABS-CBN

Duterte: So what is there in legislation that you can make that has something to do with vaccine? You cannot legislate vaccine.


GMA

Duterte: I respect the separation of powers and that you have the right to conduct Congressional investigations--in aid of legislation. Tell me now, what aid that you can legislate? When there is no goddamn vaccine until now.


GMA picked quotes that paint Duterte in a good way, while ABS-CBN picked quotes that paint Duterte in a bad way. Both approaches are wrong. The media has a responsibility to present information without bias, and let their viewers decide their own opinions.

flawed logic

Flawed logic or faulty reasoning is a way to misrepresent people’s opinions or to arrive at conclusions that are not justified by the given evidence. Flawed logic can involve jumping to conclusions or arriving at a conclusion that doesn’t follow from the premise.



In the first quote on the right, which occurred around a few days before, Duterte was referring to the army. However, in the second quote, he is referring to the PSG. Therefore, there is no evidence that the President changed his statement.

bias by omission

Bias by omission is a type of media bias in which media outlets choose not to cover certain stories or omit information that would support an alternative viewpoint.



The first quote in the example is actually made up of two quotes spliced together to make them seem more daunting. They omittted the rest of the second quote, where Duterte did not directly command Durante to ignore the Congress' summons. He said he would do that if the Congress continued the investigation. He would ask the PSG to shut up. His words were hypothetical and in future tense.

Duterte: I would ask the PSG to just shut up. Do not answer. Invoke the right against self-incrimination. At wala kayong makukuha. Durante do not obey the summons. I am ordering you to stay put in the barracks.

GMA

Duterte: Gusto ninyo ng gulo, sige. I think now, I will tell Durante, "Durante, do not obey the summons. I am ordering you to stay put in the barracks."
